Reinventing the Workflow of a Design Systems Team with Abstract

April 6, 2019

Life Before Abstract

As a designer who’s responsible for maintaining a design system’s UI library in Sketch, I’m always looking for ways to make my life easier. The complications begin with the need to manage two files: a file that’s shared among our immediate team, and another file that’s distributed to product teams within or outside SAP. Keeping track of what color has been updated, which text label has a new padding, and which component is partially released has been overwhelming, but updating the files accordingly is another pain.

Until one day I heard about Abstract, a version control tool with other features such as collaboration and developer hand-off, I downloaded it immediatly and wished that I could have known about it earlier. I ended up taking the lead to reinvent our workflow as a design systems team.

One Source of Truth

Before using Abstract, our team lacked a single source of truth for our design files. We used shared folders for everything. A folder for specifications, a folder for work-in-progress sketch files, a folder for images we use in our guidelines articles, a folder for archived files and many more. This process seemed to work for a while until the team started having new and previous members, and our design system grew to having a total of 60+ components with 3 release cycles every year. Not to mention the small bugs we fix by updating the specifications… At some point, I realized what we were doing was simply not scalable.

Abstract came in at the right time to simplify our complex workflow. Instead of having multiple shared folders that depend on everyone to upload files manually, we create and edit our files directly in Abstract. This removes the dependency on relying on everyone to upload their files. As the team grows, we can easily know where previous members’ files are located, and new members can have a better onboarding experience.

Collaborating As a Team

Our team’s collaboration has also been a lot smoother since using Abstract. Each of us may create our own branch if we would like to make a change to the master files. Abstract keeps track of our changes and makes it visible to everyone, promoting transparency across the team. One difference between the workflow of our team and product teams is that we don’t necessarily collaborate on the same file, meanwhile, we depend on each other’s work. Sometimes we have to pull a few components from team members to explore on a new component or pattern. By giving everyone access to all master files and working files, we are able to save a lot of time from digging around in layers of shared folders.

A Connected System with Linked Libraries

This removes the dependency on relying on everyone to upload their files. Let’s look at our filter pattern as an example. From the highlighted area on the left, you can see that the pattern is basically built by inserting symbols from linked libraries. If the filter form cell has an update for instance, the notification label on the top right corner will notify us about library updates. We will be able to pull changes directly from the linked file without having to edit multiple files. With linked libraries, we are getting closer to the goal of having everything consistent throughout our design system.

Efficiency & The End of Duplicative Work

Having our component library is a milestone for our team as now we are ready to generate secondary assets and files from it. Before using Abstract, we manage multiple folders for different purposes which leads to duplicative effort and a waste of time. I remember copying a symbol from our stencils/library Sketch file to a new file while working on image assets for guidelines. Later on, I realized I have been using the incorrect text color and thus the symbol in all related files need to be updated one at a time. Being able to contribute to one source of truth as a team provides us an efficient way to push and pull updates across all our files.

After setting up our component library as a “project” in Abstract, we created other projects for guidelines images, specifications and stencils. The idea is to link the libraries from the component library project to the rest of the projects. Below is a screenshot of our guidelines project where you can see the “Profile Header - Guidelines” file has three linked libraries: object and profile header from the component project, and an image template file.

The same idea applies to our specifications and stencils. Since our specifications involve a lot of written notes and component variations, it is still reasonable for us to have a separate project for it instead of relying on the inspect feature in Abstract only. The stencils project contains a file which is a compilation of linked symbols from our component library to distribute to product teams to consume.

Compiling Stencils

Producing stencils files have been a breeze after we switched to the new workflow. By simply inserting the symbols from the component library, the stencils files become a collection of our latest designs. There are no more duplicative symbols and text styles to clean up as everything is imported directly from our single source of truth. Our stencils are available to everyone, you can download them here.

Final Thoughts

If you are a designer and are considering to switch to the Abstract workflow, I highly recommend you to explore more on it. Although it will require you or your team some time to understand how it works and to set up your files properly, the amount of time your team could save is totally worth the effort.

I felt really accomplished after reinventing our new workflow with Abstract. It was indeed a great problem solving exercise and the results made our team’s daily life easier and more pleasurable.

I would like to give a shout-out to the Abstract team for spending time with us to answer our questions while we were evaluating the tool. You helped giving us back time to do actual design and we are thankful for it.
