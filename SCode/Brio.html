Brio

Blood Pressure Monitor for Senior
Users

Overview

I have always hoped to video-chat with my grandparents, but sadly they haven’t been able to figure out how to use a smartphone. During my last semester at San Jose State University in 2015, I decided to work on a topic related to smartphone usage of senior users for my thesis project.

With the help of my professors and a mentor, I was able to synthesize the information I received and turned insights into a mobile app that allows seniors to measure their blood pressure and medication intake.

Product Designer | BFA Thesis Project

Interaction Design, Visual Design, User Research, Prototyping

Aug—Dec 2015

Defining the Problem

To narrow down the topic I have chosen, I read articles and talked to seniors to understand their needs. I found that almost every senior keeps track of his/her blood pressure to some extend, since it is one of the most critical vital of a person’s well being.

Being called “the silent killer”, hypertension produces no symptoms at all. People affected are usually suggested to monitor their blood pressure levels frequently to prevent any related health problems. With several products out in the market, people can perform this task effortlessly. However, a significant group of users has been left out. About 53% of people over the age of 50 is affected by hypertension. Nearly two-thirds of Americans own a smartphone in 2015, while ownership levels remain relatively low among seniors.

Thesis Statement

By monitoring blood pressure levels and managing medication, hypertension can be treated and prevented more effectively. I intend to help people affected by hypertension to better monitor their health. I will focus on the usability of a certain user group, which is people over the age of 65. The end product of this thesis project will be a user-friendly mobile application, aiming to improve satisfaction of user experience in digital devices for senior users.

Research Findings

It’s a common belief that seniors don’t like to keep up with technology. From physical limitations, to their mindsets, people often misunderstood them for being stubborn and old-fashioned. From the results of surveys I passed around both physically and online, it’s surprising to me that seniors are very open to the idea of learning new technologies that maybe beneficial to their daily lives. Some features they are very interested in include:

- Track health data and send reports to doctors

- Notify family member in case of emergency

- Provide reminders about medication and appointments

They also pointed out some problems they have encountered:

- Content is too hard to read on the screen

- Not knowing where to tap on

- Features are too complicated

To sum it up, in order for senior users to use mobile apps comforatbly and confidently, products need to be designed tailored to their specific needs. A product should have simple functionalities that are discoverable and usable. It should always keep seniors’ physical conditions and emotional states in consideration, as they could be easily frustrated by a failed attempt to complete a task.

Prototype

After iterating and testing on multiple versions, the final prototype incorporated a lot of feedback from senior users, for example, include a physical device to automatically input data, a digital assistant to guide users through completing tasks, and several visual enhancements.

Greetings From an Assistant

As John launches Brio, the assistant greets him and provides him with three options to start with: measure his blood presure, view his medications or view his reports.

Wearing the Device

John decides to measure his blood pressure. The assitant shows him the proper way to wear the device. As soon as John is ready, the app prompts him to start measuring.

Measuring With Ease

John taps on the start button and the app starts measuring his blood pressure. He sees that his measurements are normal today and is glad to see himself in good shape.

Tracking Medication Intake

After taking his medication, John records the dosage he takes in the app with a few taps.

Sending a Report

A day before his doctor’s appointment, John would like to send a report to his doctor ahead of time. He is able to do so by navigating into his appointment details and select a report within the past two weeks.
